About

Why I built this

My grandfather was diagnosed with Alzheimer's a few years ago. Watching that happen to someone you love, and watching what it does to everyone around them, changed the way I think about medicine, about aging, and about what actually matters in the later years of a person's life. I'm a neuroscience major and I do research on neurodegeneration, but honestly, none of that is why I care about this. I care because I've seen it up close.

During COVID, I started a club in high school that paired teenagers with isolated seniors. Both generations were lonely, but especially the elderly. It was during that time I really understood how powerful simple human connection is, not just emotionally but physiologically. Social interaction isn't a nice-to-have for older adults. It's medicine.

Now I'm in college at Binghamton University, where I co-founded the Geriatric Care Club, and I volunteer regularly at an Alzheimer's and Dementia facility. The residents don't always remember my name but when I walk in, their faces light up like they're seeing someone familiar. That feeling never gets old. It means everything.

I kept hearing the same thing from friends: "I really want to volunteer, I just don't know where to start." And I always thought, there are so many places that need you, and it's genuinely not that hard, you just need a door to walk through. That's what GenerationConnect is. A door.

I built this because I believe we've gotten medicine backwards. We spend so much energy adding years to life and not nearly enough adding life to years. Sitting with someone for an hour, listening to a story you've heard before, showing up consistently, that's not charity. That's one of the most meaningful things you can do.

Joshua Fleischer is a junior at Binghamton University studying Integrative Neuroscience. He is a visiting research intern at Columbia University's Taub Institute studying synaptic dysfunction in Alzheimer's Disease, co-founder and president of the Binghamton Geriatric Care Club, and a former operating room assistant at Englewood Hospital.

First Time?

How to prepare for your first visit

From someone who's done it a lot, here's what actually helps.

1
You don't need to have anything special to say

The most common fear is "what will we even talk about?" The answer is: it doesn't matter. Ask them where they grew up. Ask about their family. People have lived entire lives full of stories and almost nobody asks them to share. You'll be amazed what comes out when you just listen.

2
If they have dementia, don't correct them

If a resident thinks it's 1975 or calls you by someone else's name, go with it. Correcting them causes distress and confusion. What matters is how they feel in the moment — safe, seen, and not alone. Enter their world rather than pulling them into yours.

3
They may not remember you — and that's okay

I volunteer at the same facility regularly. The residents don't always know my name. But when I walk in, their faces light up like I'm a familiar face. That reaction, even without the memory, tells you everything about what your presence means. Show up anyway.

4
Consistency matters more than duration

You don't need to stay for hours. An hour a week, reliably, is far more valuable than one long visit. Facilities and residents both benefit most from volunteers they can count on. Pick a time that actually works for your schedule so you can stick to it.

5
It'll feel awkward the first time, go back anyway

Almost everyone feels a little uncomfortable on their first visit. That's normal. The second visit is easier. The third, easier still. By the fourth you'll wonder why you waited so long to start. The hardest part really is just walking through the door the first time.
